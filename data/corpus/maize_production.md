# Maize Production (Nigeria)

## Overview
Maize is one of Nigeria's most widely grown cereals, cultivated from the forest zone to the dry Sudan savanna because it is not sensitive to day length and can fit into many cropping calendars.

## Land Preparation and Planting
- Plant as soon as land preparation is complete so the crop establishes ahead of weeds.
- Recommended spacing in the savanna zones is about 75 cm between rows and 50 cm within rows, with 2 plants per stand.
- About 15-20 kg of seed is needed per hectare. Plant 3 seeds per hole and thin to 2 plants per stand 1-2 weeks after germination.
- Always use certified seed of a recommended, locally adapted variety (e.g. streak- or downy-mildew-resistant types).

## Fertilizer
- A common blanket recommendation in the Nigerian savannas is roughly 100 kg N, 50 kg P2O5 (single super phosphate), and 50 kg K2O per hectare, split into two applications.
- First dose: apply at planting or within the first week, e.g. about 6 bags of NPK 15-15-15/ha.
- Second dose: apply urea (about 2 bags/ha) at 4-5 weeks after planting in southern/northern Guinea savanna, or 3-4 weeks for extra-early varieties in the Sudan savanna.
- Always bury fertilizer in the soil near the plant; never leave it exposed on the surface, as this wastes nutrients and can scorch leaves.

## Weed Control
- Two manual weedings are typically needed: around 2 weeks and 4-5 weeks after planting.
- A pre-emergence herbicide can be applied within 2 days of sowing where available.

## Key Pests and Diseases
- Fall armyworm is the most destructive pest (see separate fall armyworm document).
- Stem borers cause dead-heart and white-head symptoms (see stem borer document).
- Striga (witchweed) is a parasitic plant that can severely reduce yield; control with crop rotation (especially with legumes), proper fertilization, and Striga-free seed.
- Maize streak virus and gray leaf spot are common diseases; resistant varieties are the first line of defense.

## Harvest
Allow maize to dry properly in the field before harvesting to reduce storage losses and aflatoxin risk.

*Sources: IITA "Guide to Maize Production in Northern Nigeria"; USAID "Growing Maize in Nigeria"; Seed Co Nigeria Good Agronomic Practices.*
