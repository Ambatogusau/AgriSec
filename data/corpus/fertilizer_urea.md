# Urea Fertilizer

## What It Is
Urea is a concentrated nitrogen fertilizer containing about 46% nitrogen by weight — the highest nitrogen content of any common solid fertilizer, and one of the least expensive nitrogen sources available to farmers.

## How It Works
In the soil, the enzyme urease converts urea into ammonium, which plant roots can take up. This conversion happens within days, but if conditions are wrong it can lead to nitrogen loss before the plant benefits.

## Application Guidance
- Urea is most often used as a **top-dressing** (applied during crop growth) rather than a basal application, complementing an NPK basal dose.
- **Never apply urea to wet leaves** — it will stick to foliage and can cause leaf scorch.
- **Avoid applying urea just before heavy rain** — it will wash away before the plant can absorb it, wasting money and potentially polluting nearby water.
- Always incorporate (bury) urea into the soil surface rather than leaving granules exposed, to reduce loss through volatilization (urea breaking down into ammonia gas and escaping into the air).
- Application methods include broadcasting, banding (placing in a furrow near the row), and dissolving in irrigation water (fertigation) where irrigation is used.

## Timing Examples
- **Maize:** second nitrogen dose (urea) typically applied 3-5 weeks after planting depending on variety and zone.
- **Rice (lowland):** urea top-dressing commonly split into two applications, the first around 3-4 weeks and the second around 6-8 weeks after sowing/transplanting, to reduce nitrogen loss in flooded conditions.

*Sources: Afrimash urea fertilizer application guides; general agronomic fertilizer guidance for Nigerian smallholder cropping systems.*
