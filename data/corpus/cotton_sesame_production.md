# Cotton and Sesame Production (Nigeria)

## Why Grouped Together
Both are major cash crops grown predominantly in the same northern Nigerian savanna belt (Zamfara, Katsina, Kano, Kaduna, Jigawa, Sokoto, Kebbi, Bauchi), share similar pest management approaches, and are often used in rotation with each other — sesame is in fact recommended as a rotation crop specifically *for* cotton, since it helps reduce nematode populations that build up under continuous cotton.

## Cotton

### Production Zones and Varieties
- Zamfara, Katsina, Kano, and Adamawa together account for over 60% of Nigeria's cotton production.
- Released varieties from the Institute for Agricultural Research (IAR), Samaru include SAMCOT 8, 9, 10 (medium staple) and SAMCOT 11, 12, 13 (long staple), bred for different agro-ecological zones (North-east, North-west, North-central, Sudan savanna).
- Gossypium hirsutum (upland cotton, short-staple) makes up about 90% of Nigerian cotton production.

### Planting and Spacing
- Wider row spacing (roughly 38-40 inches) is standard, though narrower ridges can be used with careful space management.
- Plant 3 seeds per hole, about 1 inch deep, with roughly 4 inches between holes; thin to 2 plants per stand around 3 weeks after sowing.
- Weed every 3 weeks after sowing to keep the crop weed-free.

### Fertilizer
A documented research-trial rate for cotton in Nigeria: NPK 20:10:10 applied at a recommended rate of 100:30:30 kg/ha, applied around 4 weeks after sowing. Always confirm current rates with local extension, since recommendations are periodically revised.

### Key Pests
- **Cotton bollworm (Helicoverpa armigera, African bollworm):** the dominant economically damaging pest across cotton-growing regions; can cause severe yield loss if unmanaged.
- **Cotton stainers, aphids, jassids, thrips, whitefly:** all contribute to yield and quality loss; whitefly is additionally a disease vector (cotton leaf curl virus in other regions).
- **Balanced fertilization matters for pest pressure too** — excessive nitrogen application is documented to encourage bollworm and related pest outbreaks, so over-fertilizing isn't just a cost issue, it's a pest-risk issue.
- IPM approach: resistant varieties where available, balanced (not excessive) fertilization, regular monitoring/scouting, and farmer training on resistance management — relying on repeated chemical spraying alone risks both resistance buildup and unnecessary cost.

### Documented Farmer-Level Constraints
Surveys of cotton farmers in Katsina State found that lack of fertilizer access, insufficient spraying frequency (often due to cost), and limited market opportunities were the most commonly cited barriers — useful context that "knowing the right practice" and "being able to afford/access the inputs for it" are two different problems for many cotton farmers.

## Sesame

### Production Zones
Nigeria is among the world's significant sesame producers alongside India, China, Myanmar, Sudan, and Ethiopia, with production concentrated in similar northern savanna zones as cotton.

### Why Sesame Is a Valuable Rotation Crop
- Drought-resistant, low-input, and tolerant of insect pests and diseases relative to many alternatives — often described as a good "specialty crop" option for farmers wanting to diversify with lower risk.
- If sown after a legume, sesame benefits from residual soil nitrogen; if rotated with a cereal, there are mutual weed-management benefits.
- Composted sesame leaf residue left on the field improves soil moisture retention and binds the soil, benefiting the next crop's establishment and yield.

### Planting
- Sesame grows best on well-drained soils of moderate fertility; planting date should align with rainfall distribution and the length of the local growing season, with sufficient soil moisture needed for good germination.
- Land clearing before the onset of rains, with debris burned if the previous crop showed pest/disease symptoms, reduces carryover risk to the new sesame crop.

### Key Pests and Diseases
- **Sesame webworm (Antigastra catalaunalis)** is the most common and frequently encountered pest.
- **African bollworm** (the same pest affecting cotton) also attacks sesame — this is part of why similar pest management strategies to cotton are recommended for sesame.
- Recorded yield losses from pest pressure can reach over 40% in severe cases in some growing regions.
- **Cercospora and Fusarium** cause leaf rot, collar rot, and in some cases sudden plant death; crop rotation helps avoid carryover of these soil-borne disease problems.

### Harvest and Post-Harvest
- Harvest when about 50% of capsules have turned yellow — delaying harvest risks seed shattering and yield loss.
- Sun-dry pods promptly and avoid exposing harvested seed to rain, since moist sesame seed is highly prone to fungal infection and Aspergillus/aflatoxin contamination; store dry seed under moisture-free conditions.
