# Wheat Production (Nigeria)

## Overview
Nigeria depends heavily on imported wheat -- annual domestic consumption is over 5 million tonnes while national production has historically been under 500,000 tonnes, costing the country billions of dollars a year in imports. Wheat is grown almost entirely in the dry season under irrigation in the far north, not as a rainy-season crop like maize or rice.

## Where It's Grown
Kano and Jigawa states together account for over 70% of Nigeria's wheat production. Other significant producing states include Sokoto, Zamfara, Kebbi, Katsina, Yobe, Kaduna, Borno, Bauchi, Adamawa, Gombe, and Plateau. A pilot wet-season program also exists in Cross River State's southern highlands.

## Climate and Soil Needs
- Wheat needs a cool, dry climate -- it is planted in November/December (the harmattan period) and matures in about 120 days.
- Ideal temperature range is 15-25°C; rainfall needs are modest (450-650mm equivalent), met mainly through irrigation rather than rain.
- Well-drained loamy soil with low salt/acid content is ideal. In the Lake Chad region's vertisol (heavy clay) soils, irrigate at least twice a month; in sandier Sokoto-area soils, weekly irrigation is more typical.

## Fertilizer
- A commonly cited rate: 100-120 kg N/ha, 40 kg P2O5/ha, 40 kg K2O/ha -- roughly 6 bags/ha of NPK 15-15-15 at sowing, fully incorporated into the soil, plus about 3 bags/ha of urea as a top-dress 4-6 weeks after sowing.
- Timely application matters: delayed fertilizing measurably reduces both grain yield and quality.
- A soil test is recommended before fertilizing, since rates are a starting point, not a universal answer (see soil_testing_basics.md).

## Pests and Seed Treatment
The major pest is stem borer. Treating seed with a seed-dressing product (e.g. Apron Star or Apron Plus) before planting is a standard preventive measure rather than waiting to spray after an infestation is visible.

## Harvest
Check readiness by rubbing a wheat head between your fingers and chewing a grain -- if it cracks and softens rather than feeling hard/glassy, it's ready. Average yields for well-managed wheat in Nigeria run 2-5 tonnes/ha.

## Practical Note
Government programs (e.g. the National Agricultural Growth Scheme/Agro-Pocket dry season wheat program) periodically offer subsidized inputs in producing states -- check with the state Ministry of Agriculture for what's currently active, since these programs change year to year.

*Sources: Nigeria's National Bureau of Statistics wheat production survey; USDA FAS Grain and Feed Annual (Nigeria); agricdemy.com and nigerianfinder.com wheat farming guides.*
