# Stem Borers (Cereal Crops: Maize, Sorghum, Millet, Rice)

## Identification
Stem borers are caterpillar (larval) pests of cereal crops, concealed inside the stem where they feed on the internal plant tissue. This hidden feeding habit makes them difficult to control with contact insecticides once larvae are established inside the plant. Common species in West Africa include Sesamia calamistis, Busseola fusca, Eldana saccharina, and Chilo species.

## Symptoms
- **Dead-heart:** the central growing point dies and can be pulled out easily, a classic early symptom in young plants.
- **White-head:** the developing panicle/cob dries out and turns white due to internal stem damage at a later growth stage.
- Yield losses from stem borer attack range from about 10% to 100% depending on species, pest density, and the crop's growth stage at the time of attack.

## Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Approach
Stem borer control works best as a combination of methods rather than any single tactic:

1. **Cultural practices** (the foundation of IPM): early planting, timely destruction of crop residues after harvest (residues harbor overwintering larvae), and crop rotation.
2. **Host plant resistance:** planting varieties bred for tolerance, where available, reduces the need for chemical intervention.
3. **Biological control:** natural enemies including parasitic wasps (e.g. Cotesia flavipes, introduced in parts of Africa for Chilo partellus control) and entomopathogenic fungi (e.g. Beauveria bassiana) can suppress stem borer populations.
4. **"Push-pull" technology:** intercropping with a repellent plant (e.g. molasses grass, Desmodium) that pushes stem borer adults away from the main crop while an attractive border crop pulls them toward a trap area — also helps suppress the parasitic Striga weed.
5. **Chemical control:** most effective as a supplementary measure early in an infestation, since larvae become hard to reach with sprays once inside the stem.

## When to Act
Scout fields regularly, especially in the first weeks after emergence. Visible dead-heart symptoms in a noticeable share of plants is the signal to consider intervention before the pest population builds further.
