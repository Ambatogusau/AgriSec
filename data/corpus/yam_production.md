# Yam Production (Nigeria)

## Why It Matters
Nigeria is the world's largest yam producer, growing well over 60% of global supply on roughly 2.9 million hectares (historically valued at over $7 billion). White yam (Dioscorea rotundata) is the most popular species in Nigeria due to its superior tuber quality for food, though yellow yam and water yam are also widely grown. Nigeria sits within the West African "yam belt" alongside Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Benin, and Togo.

## Land Preparation and Planting
- Yam needs loose, well-drained soil — heavy clay or waterlogged soil prevents proper tuber formation, so land preparation (mounding or ridging) matters more for yam than for most other staples.
- Heaps and ridges are the standard planting method in Nigeria, allowing tubers to grow large and well-shaped with good drainage.
- Yam is planted from "setts" — cut pieces of seed yam tubers — rather than true seed.
- Planting is done at the start of the rainy season.
- The growing period is long: 8-12 months depending on variety, with early-maturing types ready in as little as 6 months and late-maturing types taking up to a year.

## Intercropping
Because yam's growing cycle is so long, intercropping with shorter-cycle crops is standard practice across all yam-producing regions of Nigeria — it gives the farmer income before the yam itself is ready, and the most viable combinations documented include yam/maize/cassava, yam/maize/cassava/okra, and yam/maize/egusi (melon). Intercropping with legumes specifically also improves soil fertility for the yam crop. Most tropical intercrop partners need similar N-P-K nutrients to yam (just in different amounts), so intercropping does not generally prevent normal fertilizer use.

## Staking
- Staking (providing support for the yam vine to climb) is essential for good yields — it ensures the leaf canopy gets enough sunlight exposure.
- Stakes can be live bush sticks left standing when clearing land, cut bamboo (6-8 ft is usually sufficient), or sturdy sticks/trellis systems.
- Vine training (regularly guiding the climbing vine after staking) keeps growth even and prevents tangling that reduces light exposure.

## Fertilizer
- A soil fertility test is recommended where available to determine actual fertilizer and lime requirements, since yam responds strongly to matched nutrition.
- A common approach: apply NPK (e.g. 16:8:24 grade) about two months after sprout emergence, placed 15-20 cm from the base of the plant rather than directly against it.
- A second application of potash (e.g. muriate of potash) around six months after planting supports optimum tuber bulking — potassium specifically drives tuber size and quality late in the season.

## Major Diseases
- **Anthracnose:** the most widely spread field disease of yam, causing stem dieback; particularly damaging in water yam. Control: field sanitation, early planting and staking, higher nitrogen dosage (around 90 kg/ha has been cited), and resistant varieties.
- **Yam Mosaic Virus Disease:** an aphid-transmitted virus considered to cause the most severe yield losses among yam diseases; manage through clean planting material, resistant varieties, and crop rotation.
- **Dry rot:** the most devastating *storage* disease (as opposed to a field disease) — it sharply reduces both the quantity and edible/marketable portion of stored tubers, so good drying and storage practice (see post-harvest document) matters as much as field management.
- Wood ash or fungicide treatment of seed tubers before planting/storage helps reduce these losses.

## Pests
Yam beetles, nematodes, and rodents are the major pests, damaging tubers directly and reducing both yield and storage quality.

## Harvest
Early harvest (June-August) involves cutting out the tuber while re-burying the crown/head to produce a second crop from the same plant within the season; the main harvest occurs October-January when leaves yellow and vines dry.
