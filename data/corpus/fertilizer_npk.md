# NPK Fertilizer

## What It Is
NPK refers to compound/blended fertilizers containing Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P, as P2O5), and Potassium (K, as K2O) — the three primary nutrients most crops need in largest quantity. The numbers on a bag (e.g. "15-15-15") show the percentage of N, P2O5, and K2O by weight.

## Role of Each Nutrient
- **Nitrogen (N):** drives vegetative growth and leaf/stem development; most commonly deficient nutrient in tropical soils under continuous cropping.
- **Phosphorus (P):** supports root development, flowering, and grain/pod fill; especially important for legumes and root establishment.
- **Potassium (K):** supports overall plant vigor, disease resistance, and grain quality.

## General Application Guidance
- NPK is typically applied as a **basal** (at-planting) dose, since phosphorus and potassium move slowly in the soil and need to be near the root zone early.
- Common compound grades used in Nigeria include NPK 15-15-15, 20-10-10, and 25-10-10, among others; the right grade depends on the crop and soil type.
- Always bury or incorporate NPK into the soil rather than leaving it on the surface — surface-applied fertilizer is lost to evaporation, runoff, and can scorch leaves on contact.
- A soil test (where available) gives the most accurate guidance on rates; blanket recommendations are a reasonable starting point in the absence of soil test data.

## Access in Nigeria
Government schemes (historically the Fertilizer Procurement and Distribution Program, and more recently the Growth Enhancement Support Scheme) have aimed to subsidize and improve smallholder access to fertilizer, though availability and pricing still vary by region and season. Confirm current input subsidy programs with a local agricultural extension office, as availability changes year to year.

*Sources: Federal Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development "Fertilizer Use and Management Practices for Crops in Nigeria"; various IITA crop production guides.*
