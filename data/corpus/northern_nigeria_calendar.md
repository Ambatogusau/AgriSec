# Northern Nigeria Cropping Calendar

## Why a Calendar Document Matters
Almost every crop guide in this knowledge base says some version of "plant when the rains are established" — but the actual timing varies by zone, and getting it wrong (too early or too late) is one of the most common, avoidable causes of poor yield. This document gives a single, consistent reference point.

## Climate Zones (North to South)
- **Sahel savanna** (e.g. far north of Borno, Yobe, Sokoto): shortest rainy season, roughly 3-4 months of useful rainfall.
- **Sudan savanna** (e.g. Kano, Katsina, Jigawa, parts of Borno/Yobe): dry season of 5-7 months; rains generally arrive June-September.
- **Northern Guinea savanna** (e.g. Kaduna, Bauchi, parts of Plateau): slightly longer rains than Sudan savanna, similar June-start pattern but often extending a bit longer into October.
- **Southern Guinea savanna / Middle Belt:** longer growing season, rains can begin in April-May.

## General Rainy Season Structure
- **Early rains (April-June):** maize, yam, and early/short-cycle crops are typically planted as rains begin to establish in the Middle Belt and southern parts of the north.
- **Peak rains (June-September):** the main planting and growth window for maize, rice, millet, sorghum, groundnut, and soybean across most of the Sudan/Sahel savanna.
- **Dry season (November-March):** main harvest period for rains-fed cereals; irrigated farming (fadama/lowland sites) becomes the main activity for vegetables (tomato, onion, pepper) and dry-season rice where irrigation infrastructure exists.

## Crop-Specific Planting Windows (Sudan/Northern Guinea Savanna, Typical Years)
| Crop | Typical planting window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Maize (early/extra-early varieties) | Last week of June (Sudan/N. Guinea); 1st-2nd week of July for extra-early types | Timing shifts year to year with rainfall onset |
| Rice (lowland) | May/June, by end of June in flood-prone areas | Earlier planting reduces flood/waterlogging risk at germination |
| Millet/Sorghum | With rains, typically June | Millet tolerates a later or more erratic start better than maize |
| Groundnut | May-June (North); April-May (Middle Belt) | Earlier planting reduces rosette disease risk |
| Soybean | June, alongside other rain-fed legumes | Confirm with local extension for variety-specific timing |
| Tomato/onion/pepper (irrigated) | November-February (dry season) | Lower disease/pest pressure than rainy season production |

## Why Timing Has Become Less Predictable
Climate studies of northern Nigeria show that the **onset date of the rains, more than the end date, is the main driver of year-to-year changes in growing season length** — and that onset has been trending later in parts of West and Central Africa in recent decades, alongside more frequent dry spells even within the rainy season. This means a farmer who plants strictly by calendar date (rather than waiting for the rains to genuinely establish) risks losing the crop to a false start. Cultural confirmation — waiting for consistent rainfall, not just a single early shower — remains the most reliable signal, even where seasonal forecasts are available.

## Practical Guidance to Give Farmers
- Use this calendar as a planning reference, not a fixed rule — always confirm against the actual rainfall pattern in the current season.
- A single early rain in April/May in the far north is often not the start of the real rains; planting on a false start risks seedling loss when dry conditions return.
- Where available, local extension offices and radio/SMS agromet advisories provide more precise, current-season onset guidance than a fixed historical calendar.

*Sources: peer-reviewed climatology of growing season onset/length in Northern Nigeria; CERES-Maize optimum planting date studies (Sudan and Northern Guinea Savanna trials); general Nigerian agricultural seasonal guides.*
