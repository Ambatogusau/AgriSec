# Cassava Production (Nigeria)

## Why Cassava Matters
Nigeria is Africa's largest cassava producer (around 60 million tonnes/year) and cassava provides more than half of daily calorie intake for people across many Nigerian ethnic groups. It is valued for ease of production, processability into garri (a roasted granule product that stores up to 12 months at room temperature), and as an industrial raw material.

## Planting
- Cassava is propagated from stem cuttings, not seed — farmers carry and plant healthy stem sections from a previous crop or certified clean planting material.
- Land preparation: double or triple harrowing before planting improves root development.
- Weed 3 or more times depending on agroecology and weed pressure — weeds are one of the biggest yield-limiting factors in cassava because of its relatively slow initial canopy closure.
- Intercropping cassava with a legume (e.g. groundnut, cowpea, soybean) is a long-standing practice that improves overall land-use efficiency and soil nitrogen status.

## Fertilizer
NPK fertilizer is commonly used, with exact rates depending on soil fertility status, acidity, moisture, and sunshine hours at the site — there is no single universal rate; a basal NPK application combined with legume intercropping is a widely recommended approach for smallholders without access to soil testing.

## Major Diseases (the Primary Threat to Yield)
- **Cassava Mosaic Disease (CMD):** caused by a complex of viruses transmitted by whitefly (Bemisia tabaci); one of the most severe and widespread cassava diseases in Nigeria, capable of causing yield losses up to 70% in susceptible varieties and stunting or killing plants.
- **Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD):** can cause yield losses up to 100% in susceptible varieties, and destroys the edible roots even when the rest of the plant still looks healthy above ground — meaning visual inspection of leaves alone is not enough to confirm a healthy harvest.
- **Cassava bacterial blight and cassava anthracnose** are other notable disease pressures.
- **The single most effective defense is planting disease-resistant varieties and using clean (disease-free) planting material** — improved IITA-derived varieties have shown potential yields of 49-53 tonnes/ha versus about 10 tonnes/ha for unimproved local varieties, with resistance to CMD and other major diseases built in.
- Avoid taking planting material (stem cuttings) from a field with visible mosaic symptoms (mottled, distorted leaves) — this is the main way the disease spreads farm to farm.

## Harvest and Quality
- Harvest roots only when there is a ready market or processing plan — cassava roots deteriorate very rapidly once harvested.
- Avoid excessive bruising of roots during harvest, which accelerates spoilage.
- For best quality, process roots within 48 hours of harvest.
- Dry matter content (the key trait buyers look for) is typically highest soon after the rains and during the harmattan (dry, dusty season), and lowest when the plant is recovering from stress or producing new leaf flush — timing harvest around this can improve market value.

*Sources: IITA/USAID "Growing Cassava in Nigeria"; IAEA/FAO "Cassava Production Guidelines for Food Security and Adaptation to Climate Change"; peer-reviewed CMD/CBSD studies from Nigerian cassava-growing zones.*
