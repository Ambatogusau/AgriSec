# Onion and Pepper Production (Nigeria, Irrigated/Dry-Season Vegetables)

## Where These Are Grown
Onion production in Nigeria is concentrated in the far north under irrigation: Kano, Kaduna, Jigawa, Katsina, Sokoto, Kebbi, Plateau, Bauchi, Borno, Nasarawa, and Gombe states, mostly within the Sudan and Northern Guinea savanna zones. Sokoto in particular has a long-established, large onion market (Kara market) supplying onions to cities across Nigeria, with prices typically lowest around February harvest. Pepper is grown more widely across the country but follows broadly similar fertilizer/irrigation principles where grown under irrigation in the dry season.

## Why Irrigation Timing Matters for Onion
- Onion is comparatively sensitive to water stress, and growth can be inhibited well before leaves visibly wilt — by the time stress is visible, yield has often already been affected.
- General irrigation guidance: water twice a month November-February, then weekly from March through early May, with irrigation frequency of about every 5-7 days and roughly 15-20 irrigation events total per growing season.
- Reduce watering as bulbs approach maturity (around 8 weeks onward) — excess water late in the season produces soft bulbs prone to rotting and reduces storage life.

## Soil and Fertilizer (Onion)
- Onion needs well-drained soil rich in organic matter, with good moisture retention — sandy loam soils high in humus work well, but the field must still drain properly to avoid waterlogging.
- Apply well-decomposed organic manure/compost (roughly 10-15 tonnes/ha) before planting to build organic matter and water retention.
- A soil test before fertilizer application is recommended where available, since nutrient needs vary significantly by location.
- Fertilizer is typically applied starting about 4 weeks after transplanting; sulfur and magnesium (often included in compound fertilizers or applied as foliar sprays) support bulb quality specifically.
- Stop or sharply reduce fertilizer application as bulbs mature (from about 8 weeks) to avoid soft, rot-prone bulbs.

## Pepper Fertilizer and Planting
- A complete fertilizer such as NPK 15-15-15 is commonly applied about 2 weeks after transplanting, placed in a shallow groove roughly 4-7 cm from the plant stem (not directly against it), then lightly covered with soil and watered immediately.
- A second application is typically given at around 50% flowering.
- The most economically important pepper pest is fruit fly, which directly damages the marketable fruit; field sanitation (removing fallen/infested fruit) and timely harvest reduce buildup.

## Common Constraints Reported by Farmers
Onion growers in major producing states have consistently reported two practical barriers: the high and rising cost of fertilizer, and difficulty accessing affordable, quality pesticides to manage insect pests — useful context when a farmer asks why recommended practices feel out of reach, since input cost and access (not just agronomic knowledge) are real constraints in these systems.

*Sources: NAERLS "Onion Production and Management under Irrigation"; agriculturenigeria.com onion and pepper production guides; peer-reviewed onion water footprint study (Nigeria's major producing states).*
