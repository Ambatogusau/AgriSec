# Groundnut Production (Nigeria)

## Background
Nigeria was once Africa's largest groundnut exporter, but drought and rosette disease devastated production in the 1970s. Improved, disease-resistant varieties have since been developed (e.g. Samnut series).

## Varieties
- **Samnut 22:** good pod/seed quality, resistant to aphids.
- **Samnut 24 (IAR 19BT):** high-yielding, rosette-resistant, drought-tolerant, matures in 90-100 days.
- **Samnut 25:** disease-resistant, matures in 120-130 days, suited to moderate rainfall areas.
- **Samnut 26:** early-maturing (80-90 days), highly resistant to rosette, good oil content (~51%).

## Planting
- Apply 5-10 tonnes/ha of farmyard manure before planting to improve soil structure (groundnut soils are typically sandy and low in fertility).
- Plant May-June in the North, April-May in the Middle Belt, after rains are established.
- Spacing: about 75 cm between rows x 25 cm within rows (roughly 53,000 plants/ha). Plant 5-6 cm deep, one seed per hole.
- Can be intercropped with maize, sorghum, or millet in a 1:1 row pattern.
- **Early planting and closer spacing both reduce groundnut rosette disease incidence** — this is one of the most effective and lowest-cost control measures available.

## Groundnut Rosette Disease
- A viral disease transmitted by aphids (Aphis craccivora); can cause up to 100% yield loss in severe epidemics. A historical epidemic in northern Nigeria destroyed about 0.75 million hectares.
- Control: use rosette-resistant varieties, plant early, avoid wide spacing, and manage aphid populations.

## Other Pests and Diseases
- **Aphids** (Aphis craccivora): brownish-gray insects that feed on growing tips and young foliage; vector for rosette and other viruses; can cause up to 40% yield loss directly, and are most damaging to young plants under drought stress.
- **Early and late leaf spot:** the most damaging foliar fungal diseases of groundnut worldwide; carbendazim-based fungicides can help control both.
- Integrated pest management (resistant varieties + crop rotation + sanitation + targeted chemical use) is recommended over reliance on insecticide alone.

## Harvest
Groundnut matures 120-150 days after planting. Harvest when leaves yellow and inner pod ribs darken; dry pods under shade to 8-10% moisture before shelling and storage to limit fungal growth and aflatoxin risk.

*Sources: ICRISAT/IITA "A Farmer's Guide to Profitable Groundnut Production in Nigeria"; A'Brook (1964) on planting date/spacing and rosette incidence.*
