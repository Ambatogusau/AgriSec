# Fish Farming / Aquaculture (Nigeria)

## Why It's Relevant to an Agricultural Assistant
Fish farming is one of the fastest-growing parts of Nigerian agriculture, and demand significantly outstrips domestic supply — Nigeria's fish demand is estimated at around 2.66 million metric tonnes/year against domestic production of about 1.4 million tonnes, with the gap filled by imports of over 500,000 tonnes annually. This makes aquaculture a genuine, growing income opportunity for smallholders, not just a niche activity.

## Main Species
- **Catfish (Clarias, hybrid, and heterobranchus types):** the most widely farmed species in Nigeria, valued for hardiness — tolerates a wide range of water quality conditions including low oxygen and high turbidity, and can be stocked more densely than tilapia, which generally means higher yield per pond.
- **Tilapia:** the second most popular farmed fish; requires cleaner, warmer, well-oxygenated water and more space per fish than catfish, with closer monitoring needed to avoid stress and disease. Despite being more demanding to manage, some farmers report tilapia is ultimately more profitable due to lower feed costs and strong/recovering market demand.

## Production System Types
- **Extensive:** low labor/input cost, relies mostly on natural pond food production; lower productivity, sometimes boosted with basic fertilizer.
- **Semi-intensive:** moderate inputs — fertilizer and/or supplementary feeding — higher labor and feed cost but meaningfully higher yield.
- **Intensive:** high stocking density and full supplementary feeding; highest input cost and yield, more capital-intensive to start.
- Common pond types in Nigeria include earthen ponds, concrete ponds, tarpaulin/household systems, and (increasingly) cage culture in natural water bodies.

## Key Management Practices
- **Water quality monitoring is the single most important ongoing task**, especially for tilapia; test kits for water chemistry are a worthwhile investment even for smallholder operations.
- **Sort fish by size regularly** — larger fish can outcompete or even prey on smaller fish in the same pond if size classes are mixed, so separating by size into different ponds/tanks protects growth rates of smaller fish.
- **Biosecurity matters as much in aquaculture as in poultry/livestock** — water source quality, avoiding contamination between ponds, and careful broodstock/fingerling sourcing reduce disease introduction.
- Early growth stages carry the highest mortality risk, particularly for tilapia fry — this is the stage where farmer training and close monitoring have the largest impact on overall production outcomes.

## Practical Notes for New Farmers
- Good quality water supply and a known-origin, fast-growing broodstock/fingerling source are the two most commonly cited foundational requirements for starting a viable operation.
- Record-keeping (stocking dates, feeding amounts, mortality, water quality readings) is repeatedly highlighted by experienced farmers and extension programs as a practice that separates profitable operations from struggling ones.

*Sources: WorldFish Nigeria aquaculture program reports (GIFT tilapia initiatives); Afrimash catfish and fish farming guides; Nigerian aquaculture industry overviews.*
