# Striga (Witchweed) Management

## Why This Deserves Its Own Document
Striga is mentioned as a risk factor in the maize, rice, millet/sorghum, and groundnut documents in this knowledge base, but the scale of the problem justifies a dedicated, detailed reference: Striga infests an estimated 50 million hectares of cereal cropland in sub-Saharan Africa, causes roughly US$7 billion in annual losses, and affects the livelihoods of over 300 million people in the region. A specific study in the savanna zones of north-east Nigeria documented how farmers' own crop management choices directly influence how bad an infestation gets — meaning farmer practice, not just soil or luck, is a major factor.

## How Striga Damages Crops
Striga is a parasitic plant that attaches to the roots of host cereals (maize, sorghum, millet, upland rice) underground, siphoning off water, nutrients, and sugars before it ever becomes visible above ground. By the time the purple/red-flowered Striga plant is visible in the field, the host crop has often already suffered significant hidden root damage — twisted, discolored, and stunted growth follow. In severe cases, infestation causes total crop failure and farmers abandon affected fields entirely.

## Why Poor Soil Makes It Worse
There is a strong, well-documented link between soil fertility depletion and the severity of Striga damage: the less available nutrients in a field, the more aggressively the parasite attacks the host crop. This means **soil fertility management is itself a core Striga control strategy**, not just a separate concern.

## Integrated Management Options (Combine Several — No Single Method Is Sufficient)
1. **Fertilization (organic + mineral):** improving soil fertility directly reduces Striga damage severity, independent of any other control method.
2. **Resistant/tolerant varieties:** IITA and partners have released Striga-tolerant maize varieties for West Africa; in northeast Nigeria, improved varieties have shown yields of 1-2 tonnes/ha versus 0.3-0.5 tonnes/ha for local varieties under farmer management — a substantial, well-documented difference.
3. **Crop rotation and intercropping with legumes:** rotating or intercropping with non-host legumes (soybean, groundnut, cowpea) breaks the Striga life cycle and improves soil nitrogen simultaneously.
4. **"Push-pull" technology:** intercropping the main cereal with desmodium (a legume groundcover) suppresses Striga germination near the host crop while also improving soil fertility — the same technique used against stem borers, giving it a useful dual benefit.
5. **Trap crops:** in heavily infested fields, growing a crop like Crotalaria that stimulates Striga seed germination without allowing successful attachment can help deplete the Striga seed bank over time.
6. **Early and thorough weeding/hand-pulling:** removing Striga plants before they flower and set seed prevents replenishing the soil seed bank, which can persist for years.
6. **Biological control:** a fungal biocontrol agent for Striga hermonthica has been developed by IITA and partners as a lower-cost, accessible tool for smallholders (described in IITA's "Striga Biocontrol on a Toothpick" approach for low-cost application).
7. **Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides:** can be effective but require correct product selection, since Striga's parasitic relationship with the host crop makes general weed herbicides less reliable than targeted approaches.

## Practical Guidance for Farmers
- If Striga has appeared in a field before, the most cost-effective immediate steps are: improve fertilization, rotate with a legume next season, and hand-pull any Striga plants before they flower.
- Avoid replanting the same susceptible cereal in a heavily infested field without addressing soil fertility and rotation first — yield in an infested field under poor management can approach total loss.
- A Striga-resistant or -tolerant variety is one of the highest-leverage single changes available where infestation is already established, given the documented yield gap versus local varieties.

*Sources: IITA "The Witch is Dead" (biocontrol); Dugje, Kamara & Omoigui (2006, 2008) studies on Striga infestation in northeast Nigeria savanna zones; IITA "Proactive Management of Striga Infestation"; peer-reviewed Striga management trials in sub-Saharan Africa.*
