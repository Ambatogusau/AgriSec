# Soil Testing Basics

## Why This Document Exists
Nearly every crop document in this knowledge base says some version of "confirm with a soil test where available" before applying a blanket fertilizer rate. This document explains what that actually means in practice, because most smallholder farmers in Nigeria currently apply fertilizer without reference to their soil's specific needs — research describes this as a widespread problem that limits how much farmers actually benefit from the fertilizer they buy.

## Why Blanket Rates Are a Compromise, Not the Ideal
The fertilizer rates given throughout this knowledge base (e.g. "6 bags of NPK 15-15-15 per hectare") are blanket recommendations — reasonable starting points developed from research trials across many fields, but not tailored to any specific farmer's soil. Actual nutrient deficiencies vary significantly field to field, even within the same village, depending on soil type, cropping history, and prior fertilizer/manure use. A 2020-era multi-state review of Nigerian soils found widespread deficiencies not just in nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium but also in micronutrients like copper, zinc, boron, calcium, and sulfur — nutrients that blanket NPK recommendations don't address at all.

## Three Ways to Assess Soil Fertility (From Least to Most Accurate)
1. **Visual symptom diagnosis:** observing plant symptoms (yellowing, stunting, poor fruit set) to guess at a deficiency. Useful as an early warning sign, but symptoms often overlap between different nutrient deficiencies and can be confused with disease or water stress.
2. **Plant tissue analysis:** lab testing of plant leaf/tissue samples during the growing season to measure actual nutrient uptake. More accurate than visual diagnosis but requires lab access and only tells you about the current season, too late to change that season's basal fertilizer decision.
3. **Soil testing (most reliable):** lab analysis of a soil sample taken before planting, directly measuring available nutrients, pH, and organic matter. This is the only method that lets a farmer adjust fertilizer choices *before* the season starts.

## How Soil Testing Works in Practice
- A representative soil sample (typically several small samples from across a field, mixed together, from the top 15-20 cm of soil) is sent to a laboratory.
- The lab reports nutrient levels (N, P, K, and often micronutrients and pH) and, often, a specific fertilizer recommendation matched to the crop being grown.
- The goal is "site-specific nutrient management" — applying the right nutrient, at the right rate, right time, and right place (the "4R" principle referenced in some of the maize/soybean research cited elsewhere in this knowledge base) — rather than a one-size-fits-all rate.

## Where Nigerian Farmers Can Access Soil Testing
- **State Agricultural Development Programs (ADPs)** and extension offices in many states can advise on or facilitate soil sample collection and testing.
- **University and research institute soil science laboratories** (e.g. at agricultural universities and IITA) offer testing services, sometimes through extension partnerships.
- **Private commercial labs** with Nigerian operations exist (e.g. labs with an Ibadan-based office serving Nigerian farmers) and can provide soil nutrient analysis plus a tailored fertilizer program; cost and turnaround time should be weighed against farm size and crop value.
- Decision-support tools used in some IITA/TAMASA-linked programs (e.g. "Nutrient Expert") combine field/soil information with crop-specific algorithms to generate a tailored fertilizer recommendation without necessarily requiring a full lab soil test — extension officers in maize-focused programs may have access to these tools.

## Practical Advice for a Farmer Without Easy Lab Access
- Use the blanket rates in this knowledge base as a starting point, not a final answer.
- Watch for visual deficiency symptoms (e.g. general yellowing suggesting nitrogen deficiency, purplish leaf tinge sometimes suggesting phosphorus deficiency) as an early signal that the current fertilizer program may need adjustment — but treat this as a rough signal, not a diagnosis.
- Where a soil test genuinely isn't accessible, applying organic manure alongside inorganic fertilizer (see the organic manure document) is a reasonably low-risk way to broaden nutrient coverage beyond N-P-K alone, since manure contributes a wider range of micronutrients along with organic matter.
- Ask the local extension office directly whether any soil testing or decision-support program (such as Nutrient Expert) is currently active in the area — availability changes over time and by region, and an extension officer will know the current status better than a general guide can.
