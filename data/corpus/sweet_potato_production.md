# Sweet Potato Production (Nigeria)

## Overview
Sweet potato is a widely grown root crop across Nigeria, valued for being relatively low-input and tolerant of poorer soils compared to many other staples. It is grown from vine cuttings, not true seed -- a farmer typically takes healthy vine cuttings from a previous crop or a multiplication plot.

## Fertilizer -- A Different Story From Most Crops
Unlike maize, rice, or tomato, **most Nigerian sweet potato farmers traditionally do not apply fertilizer at all**, relying instead on residual soil fertility. Research shows this leaves real yield on the table: combining conventional tillage with NPK fertilizer and poultry manure has been shown to give the highest tuber yield in controlled trials, while also improving soil structure for the following crop. Where it's accessible, a moderate NPK application plus organic manure is a meaningfully better practice than the traditional no-fertilizer approach, not just a marginal improvement.

## Weeding
A second hoe-weeding after initial establishment is specifically recommended to prevent weeds from interfering with tuber bulking, since sweet potato's below-ground growth is more sensitive to competition than its modest above-ground appearance suggests.

## Harvest
- Harvest occurs 3-8 months after planting depending on variety.
- Harvest involves cutting off the vines first, then carefully digging out tubers (fork shovel, long wooden sticks, or hoes) while avoiding bruising -- bruised tubers spoil much faster.
- **Harvest at the earliest point of maturity rather than waiting** -- delaying harvest increases the risk of weevil (beetle) attack as the soil dries out around the tubers and they become more exposed to pest pressure.
- Harvest timing is flexible and can be staggered, digging up only what's needed and leaving the rest in the ground temporarily, which is a practical advantage over crops that must be harvested all at once.

## Economic Note
Sweet potato has grown into a real cash crop in Nigeria over the last two decades, alongside cassava, yam, oil palm, maize, sorghum, millet, and rice as one of the country's significant agricultural production-value crops.

*Sources: EPAR (Evans School Policy Analysis and Research) "Sweet Potato Value Chain: Nigeria"; Agbede and Adekiya (2011) tillage/fertilizer trial findings.*
