# Millet and Sorghum Production (Nigeria)

## Why These Crops Matter
Pearl millet and sorghum are central to food security in the drier parts of Nigeria (e.g. Borno and Adamawa states, where over half of cropland can be devoted to millet). Both tolerate drought, soil salinity, soil acidity, and high temperatures better than maize or rice, making them resilient choices in the Sudan and Sahel savanna zones.

## Cropping Systems
- Often grown as an intercrop with legumes such as groundnut or cowpea, or mixed with other cereals like sorghum and maize — this spreads risk and improves soil fertility through legume nitrogen fixation.
- Sorghum is commonly intercropped late-season with vegetables (tomato, onion) in the North-West, or with tuber crops like yam in the North-Central zone.

## Planting
- Pearl millet: standard spacing is roughly 75 cm x 75 cm under normal conditions, widened to 75 cm x 100 cm or 50 cm x 100 cm under very low fertility/dry conditions. Sow 5-8 seeds per hill, thin to 2 plants/stand after a good rain.
- Sorghum: plant on a well-prepared seed bed as soon as rains are established; thin 2-3 weeks after planting.

## Fertilizer
- Because these crops are often grown by farmers with limited cash for inputs, **fertilizer micro-dosing** (applying small, precisely placed amounts of fertilizer directly at the seed/plant) is a widely promoted, low-cost technique that improves nutrient efficiency compared to broadcasting.
- For sorghum, apply the first dose of fertilizer about 3 weeks after sowing.
- Where blanket recommendations are used, rates are broadly similar in magnitude to maize but should be adjusted downward in the driest zones to match crop water availability.

## Weed and Pest Management
- Striga (witchweed) is a major parasitic weed problem in both crops; control through crop rotation, resistant varieties, and proper fertilization (Striga is worse on poor soils).
- Stem borers affect both crops similarly to maize (see stem borer document).

## Climate Resilience Practices
Millet-legume intercropping, agroforestry, crop rotation, fertilizer micro-dosing, and water harvesting are the key interventions promoted across the Sahel to sustain yields under increasing climate stress.
