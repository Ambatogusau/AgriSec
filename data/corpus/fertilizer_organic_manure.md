# Organic Manure

## What It Is
Organic manure includes farmyard/animal manure, composted crop residues, and other organic matter applied to improve soil fertility and structure. Unlike inorganic fertilizers (NPK, urea), organic manure releases nutrients slowly as it decomposes, and also improves soil's ability to hold water and nutrients over time.

## Benefits
- Improves soil structure, especially valuable on the sandy, low-fertility soils common in groundnut- and millet-growing areas of northern Nigeria.
- Increases organic carbon and helps buffer soil pH.
- Reduces dependence on purchased inputs, which matters for smallholders facing high or volatile fertilizer prices.
- Can be combined with inorganic fertilizer (an "organomineral" approach) — research in Nigeria has shown that combining urea with farmyard manure (e.g. a 75% urea-N + 25% manure-N split) can outperform either input used alone for maize and rice yield.

## Practical Use
- Apply organic manure or compost before planting and incorporate it into the soil during land preparation, rather than applying it on top of an already-established crop.
- For groundnut specifically, be cautious: excessive organic manure applied during the cropping season (rather than before planting) has been associated with increased white grub infestation in some studies — apply well before planting and allow time for partial decomposition.
- Composting crop residues (turning the heap every two weeks) before field application improves nutrient availability compared to using fresh, undecomposed material.
- Rice straw can be incorporated directly into flooded fields and left to decompose for about 30 days before mixing into the soil and applying fertilizer — this improves water and nutrient retention, especially on sandy soils.

*Sources: Federal Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development fertilizer guidance; IITA rice and groundnut production guides; Nigerian agronomic research on organomineral fertilizer combinations.*
