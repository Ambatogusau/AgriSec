# Oil Palm Production (Nigeria)

## Overview
Nigeria is among the world's leading oil palm producers (alongside Malaysia and Indonesia), and the crop is a long-term investment -- a mature oil palm plantation can produce fresh fruit bunches for up to 25 years once established, making it one of the more profitable long-term agricultural ventures available, but also one requiring patience: trees take several years to begin fruiting.

## Varieties
- **Dura:** the traditional, local open-pollinated variety long planted by Nigerian farmers. Lower oil yield, takes 6-8 years to fruit, but has useful resistance to some locally prevalent pests and diseases.
- **Tenera:** the most popular hybrid variety in Nigeria today, combining higher oil yield with improved disease resistance compared to Dura.

## Planting
- A triangular planting layout, with palms spaced about 9 meters apart in each direction, is the standard recommendation -- this spacing prevents competition for light, water, and nutrients as the trees mature. At this spacing, roughly 145 seedlings fit per hectare.
- Dig planting holes several days before transplanting; have well-rotted manure ready.
- Place seedlings vertically in the hole, then fill with more manure than soil.
- Add roughly 400-450 grams of single super phosphate fertilizer to each planting hole, along with microbial inoculants if available, to help young seedlings establish.

## Fertilizer (Ongoing, Mature Trees)
- Oil palm is a heavy feeder and needs balanced macro-, secondary-, and micro-nutrients (including nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium, boron, and sulfur) throughout its productive life, not just at planting.
- A common compound fertilizer ratio for established palms is NPK+Mg in a 12:12:17:2 ratio, applied in split doses roughly every three months, spread in a ring about 50-75 cm away from the trunk (never piled against it, to avoid scorching).
- Apply fertilizer in four equal split doses per year, commonly starting around June/July.
- Adding 50-100 kg of well-rotted farmyard manure per palm alongside a fertilizer dose further improves results, especially on poorer soils.

## Pests and Disease Management
- Main threats include rats, leaf-eating insects, rhinoceros beetles, and stem rot.
- Low-cost preventive measures: keep the plantation clean by removing rotting palm trunks (which attract rhinoceros beetles), maintain a healthy soft weed/legume groundcover rather than bare soil, and preserve natural predators like snakes that help control rat populations rather than eliminating them.
- If nematodes are confirmed in the soil, a suitable nematicide should be applied before or at planting.

## Practical Constraints Worth Knowing
Access to quality planting material and fertilizer, delays in input delivery, and limited extension/market information are commonly cited real-world barriers to smallholder oil palm productivity in Nigeria -- not just agronomic knowledge gaps.
