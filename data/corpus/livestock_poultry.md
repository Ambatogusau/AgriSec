# Poultry (Broilers and Layers)

## Broiler vs Layer
- **Broiler:** raised for meat, fast turnaround (typically 6-8 weeks to market weight).
- **Layer:** raised for egg production; goes through a pullet-rearing phase (0-18 weeks) before a long laying phase (18-72+ weeks), with spent hens sold at the end as a secondary product.

## Major Diseases
- **Newcastle Disease (ND):** highly contagious viral disease causing respiratory distress, diarrhea, and neurological signs; one of the most economically damaging poultry diseases in Nigeria, capable of causing catastrophic mortality in unvaccinated flocks within days.
- **Infectious Bursal Disease / Gumboro:** attacks the immune system, increasing susceptibility to other infections.
- **Infectious Bronchitis:** viral, causes respiratory symptoms and reduced egg production/quality in layers.
- **Coccidiosis:** parasitic intestinal disease causing weight loss and bloody diarrhea; managed with anticoccidial medication, especially during brooding.
- **Marek's Disease:** typically vaccinated at the hatchery (included in day-old-chick price from reputable hatcheries).

## Typical Vaccination Timing (confirm exact products/timing with a local vet or hatchery, as schedules vary by region and disease prevalence)
- **Days 5-7:** Newcastle Disease + Infectious Bronchitis (often combined, via eye drop or drinking water).
- **Around day 14:** Gumboro/IBD vaccine (oral, via drinking water).
- **Around day 21:** Newcastle Disease booster.
- Layers receive additional booster doses through the laying period (e.g. fowl pox via wing-web puncture, Coryza, Egg Drop Syndrome) given their much longer productive life compared to broilers.

## Vaccine Handling
Keep vaccines refrigerated on the farm, separate from food or chemicals. When transporting, use a cool box with ice packs and avoid direct sunlight — vaccine potency is highly sensitive to heat exposure.

## Biosecurity (Low-Cost, High-Impact)
- Restrict access to poultry housing to authorized people only.
- Use a footbath/disinfectant at the entrance to the poultry house (a measure many smallholder farms skip, but one of the cheapest ways to reduce disease introduction).
- Keep different age groups of birds separated where possible.

## Why Vaccination Adherence Matters
Studies in Nigeria show that many rural smallholders either skip vaccination or don't follow standard procedures, which both risks vaccine failure and creates reservoirs for endemic diseases like ND and IBD that can then spread to vaccinated flocks nearby. Consistent, correctly-timed vaccination protects not just an individual flock but neighboring farms too.

*Sources: Afrimash poultry vaccination guides; peer-reviewed Nigerian poultry disease surveillance studies (Oyo and Kano states); smallholder broiler practice studies (Aguata, Nigeria).*
