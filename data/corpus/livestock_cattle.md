# Cattle

## Common Breeds in Nigeria
- **White Fulani:** widely kept indigenous breed, dual-purpose (meat/milk), adapted to local conditions.
- **Sokoto Gudali, Red Bororo, Keteku:** other indigenous breeds with varying disease susceptibility profiles documented in regional studies.
- Crossbreeding (e.g. White Fulani x Jersey) is sometimes used to improve milk quality and yield while retaining some local adaptation, though exotic genetics generally require better feeding and management to perform well.

## Major Diseases
Pastoralist communities (e.g. Fulani herders) have detailed traditional knowledge of cattle disease, often using local-language names:

- **Trypanosomiasis ("sammore"):** a parasitic disease transmitted by tsetse flies; one of the most commonly diagnosed parasitic diseases in surveyed cattle populations in Nigeria.
- **Foot and Mouth Disease ("boro"):** a highly contagious viral disease; serological studies show substantial historical exposure in some cattle populations, varying by breed.
- **Contagious Bovine Pleuropneumonia / CBPP ("hanta"):** a serious bacterial respiratory disease.
- **Liver fluke, parasitic gastro-enteritis, tick-borne diseases (e.g. babesiosis), and mange** are also commonly identified in veterinary surveys, with tick-borne disease prevalence sometimes underestimated by pastoralists' own disease reports compared to parasitological survey findings.

## Management Priorities
- **Regular vaccination and veterinary visits** are repeatedly identified as the most important step to reduce losses — vaccines for several major endemic diseases (PPR, CBPP, lumpy skin disease, Newcastle disease for any co-located poultry) are registered and available through national distribution efforts.
- **Tick and tsetse fly control** (e.g. through regular dipping/spraying, pasture management, and avoiding known fly-belt grazing areas during peak season) reduces the two most consistently reported parasitic disease burdens.
- Because pastoral and smallholder cattle keepers often have limited access to veterinary services, building a relationship with a local veterinary officer or community animal health worker — even for periodic check-ins rather than only emergency treatment — meaningfully improves disease detection timing.

## Practical Note on Disease Reporting
When describing symptoms to an extension officer or vet, local disease names (e.g. Hausa/Fulfulde terms used by pastoralist communities) are often well-established and clinically meaningful — sharing these terms accurately helps faster diagnosis, but should always be confirmed against current veterinary guidance since traditional and clinical disease categories don't always map one-to-one.

*Sources: peer-reviewed participatory epidemiology studies of Fulani pastoralist cattle disease knowledge (North-East Nigeria); FMD seroprevalence study (Bauchi State); Nigeria Federal Ministry of Information livestock disease initiatives (2026).*
