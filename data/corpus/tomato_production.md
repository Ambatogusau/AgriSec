# Tomato Production (Nigeria)

## Overview
Nigeria is Africa's largest tomato producer, with the Northern region (Kano, Kaduna, Sokoto, Katsina, Zamfara, Bauchi, Taraba) as the main production zone. Tomato grows almost anywhere in Nigeria with adequate water, but does better in the savanna than the humid forest zone because pest and disease pressure is lower there. Average national yield has historically been below 3 tonnes/ha, well under potential, largely due to poor seed selection, pest pressure, and post-harvest losses.

## Nursery and Transplanting
- Raise seedlings in a nursery bed or seedling trays before transplanting — do not direct-seed into the main field.
- Seedlings are ready to transplant at 4-5 leaves, typically about 4 weeks after sowing, when they reach roughly 8-10 cm tall.
- Transplant in the late afternoon or on a cloudy day to reduce transplant shock; water immediately after transplanting.
- Apply farmyard manure to the field about 2-3 weeks before transplanting to allow it to begin improving soil structure and organic matter.

## Spacing
A common recommendation is two rows per 1-meter bed, 60 cm between rows, 40 cm between plants — roughly 33,000 plants/ha. Stake plants grown in the rainy season to keep fruit off wet soil and improve air circulation.

## Fertilizer
- NPK 15-15-15 is commonly used, at rates around 200-250 kg/ha split into two equal doses, placed about 5 cm from the plant (the "ring method") rather than directly against the stem.
- **Never apply fertilizer to young or wet plants — it causes burns.**
- Tomato needs phosphorus especially after transplanting (root establishment) and benefits from calcium during flowering and potassium during fruiting to reduce blossom end rot and improve fruit quality.
- Water after fertilizer application to help it dissolve and move into the root zone.

## Major Pest: Tomato Leaf Miner (Tuta absoluta)
This is the single most damaging tomato pest in Nigeria today, and the reason for the country's major tomato price crises in 2016 and subsequent years.
- Larvae tunnel into stems, buds, and both green and ripe fruit, directly destroying the harvestable product rather than just damaging leaves.
- Reported yield losses of 80-100% in severe, unmanaged outbreaks.
- **The pest has developed resistance to several commonly used insecticides in Nigeria** (including pyrethroids like λ-cyhalothrin) — spraying the same chemical repeatedly is often why control fails, not insufficient spraying.
- Insecticide research in northern Nigeria found abamectin-based products gave the highest mortality among tested options, while resistance was already established to several older chemical classes; always check current local extension guidance, since resistance patterns shift with use.
- IPM approaches recommended by NIHORT (National Horticultural Research Institute) include pheromone/tray trapping to monitor and reduce adult populations, crop rotation away from Solanaceae (tomato, pepper, potato) family crops, and field sanitation (removing and destroying infested plant material) — chemical control alone is increasingly unreliable due to resistance.
- Biological control using natural enemies (parasitic wasps) is used successfully in protected/greenhouse production elsewhere and is an area of active research for open-field Nigerian conditions.

## Other Pests and Diseases
- Bacterial wilt and bacterial spot are recurring disease issues; crop rotation (e.g. with paddy rice) breaks the disease cycle since these pathogens persist in soil between tomato crops.
- Nematodes can be reduced by pre-planting soil treatment in nursery beds.

## Timing
Dry season (roughly November-February) planting is often preferred commercially because disease pressure is lower, provided irrigation is available; rainy season tomato is more disease-prone but does not require irrigation investment.

*Sources: NIHORT (National Horticultural Research Institute) "Production Guide for Tomatoes"; peer-reviewed Tuta absoluta studies from northern Nigeria (insecticide resistance bioassays); Nigeria tomato leaf miner invasion review (2018).*
