# Desert Locusts

## Why Locusts Are Different From Other Pests
The desert locust (Schistocerca gregaria) is a transboundary migratory pest — swarms move across national borders and can travel long distances, eating along the way. A single one-square-kilometer swarm can contain up to 80 million adult locusts, capable of eating as much food in a day as roughly 35,000 people would consume. Because of this, locust control depends heavily on early detection and coordinated regional/national action, not just field-level measures.

## Early Warning Is the Most Important Defense
- The FAO Desert Locust Information Service (DLIS) monitors weather, ecological conditions, and locust reports globally, issuing forecasts and warnings for affected countries.
- Tools like the mobile app eLocust3 (and eLocust3m for community reporting) let field staff and even farmers/herders report sightings in real time via satellite or mobile networks, feeding directly into the early warning system.
- **If you see locust nymphs (hoppers, wingless young) or adult groups/bands forming, report it immediately to local agricultural extension or plant protection authorities** — early-stage outbreaks are far easier to contain than established swarms.

## Field-Level Response
- The best time to spray locust swarms is early morning or late afternoon, when locusts are settled on the ground or vegetation rather than in flight.
- Biopesticides and insect growth regulators are increasingly used as more environmentally friendly alternatives to broad-spectrum chemical pesticides, particularly important given that swarms often move through areas with people, livestock, and water sources.
- Locust control at the field level is usually carried out by trained government/regional teams rather than individual farmers, due to the scale and coordination required, but farmer reporting is what triggers timely action.

## Regional Coordination
Bodies such as the Desert Locust Control Commission for the Region (CLCPRO) in North-West Africa coordinate cross-border surveillance and rapid response, since locust breeding conditions and swarm movement do not respect national boundaries.

*Sources: FAO Desert Locust Information Service (DLIS); FAO Locust Watch program materials; FAO regional outbreak reporting (2025-2026).*
