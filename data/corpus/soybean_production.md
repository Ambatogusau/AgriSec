# Soybean Production (Nigeria)

## Why Inoculation Matters
Soybean yield in Nigeria is often limited not by the variety itself but by poor nodulation with native soil rhizobium bacteria, low phosphorus availability, and weak crop management. Soybean forms a partnership with rhizobium bacteria that fix nitrogen from the air into a usable form — but the right rhizobium strain must be present in the soil, especially for "non-promiscuous" varieties bred outside Africa.

## Inoculation (Key Practice)
- IITA developed promiscuous soybean varieties (crossing Asian-origin promiscuous types with higher-yielding US varieties) that nodulate reasonably well with native Nigerian soil bacteria, but **inoculation with a quality rhizobium product still substantially increases yield**, even in promiscuous varieties.
- A widely used product is **NoduMax**, a Bradyrhizobium japonicum inoculant developed by IITA, available across Nigeria, Benin, Ghana, and Togo.
- General application: dissolve the sticker agent in warm water, mix with seed, then mix in the inoculant powder; use roughly 10g of inoculant per kg of seed (confirm exact rates with the product label or an agro-dealer, since instructions vary by brand).
- Combining inoculation with phosphorus (P) fertilizer gives a stronger yield response than either input alone.

## Yield Potential
With good practices (right variety, inoculation, adequate P), sole-cropped soybean grain yield can reach 2,500-3,000 kg/ha.

## Other Notes
- Soybean is valuable beyond grain: it provides a cholesterol-free vegetable oil, soybean cake is an excellent poultry feed, and the haulms (residue) make good feed for sheep and goats.
- Crop rotation with soybean also benefits subsequent cereal crops (maize, sorghum) through residual soil nitrogen.

*Sources: IITA "Guide to Soybean Production in Northern Nigeria"; N2Africa project materials; peer-reviewed studies on rhizobium inoculation response in northern Nigeria.*
