# Aphids (Groundnut, Legumes, and Other Crops)

## Identification
Aphids (e.g. Aphis craccivora, the groundnut/cowpea aphid) are small, soft-bodied, brownish-gray insects. They are "polyphagous," meaning they feed on many different plant species. Both adult and nymph (young) stages feed mostly on growing tips and young foliage.

## Why Aphids Matter Beyond Direct Damage
Aphids are most damaging not just through direct feeding but as **disease vectors**: they transmit groundnut rosette virus, peanut mottle virus, and peanut stripe virus. Aphid feeding alone can cause yield losses of up to 40% in groundnut, and aphid-transmitted rosette disease can cause near-total crop loss in epidemic years.

## When Aphids Are Most Damaging
Aphids are sporadic (periodic) pests but attack crops at all growth stages. They cause the most serious damage when the crop is young and under drought stress, when plants have less capacity to recover from feeding damage.

## Management
- **Early planting and closer plant spacing** reduce both aphid landing rates and disease (rosette) incidence — this is a well-documented, low-cost cultural control.
- **Resistant varieties** (e.g. several Samnut groundnut varieties bred with aphid/rosette resistance) are an effective long-term strategy.
- **Biological control:** natural predators such as ladybirds (ladybugs) and parasitic wasps help suppress aphid populations and can be encouraged through habitat management rather than broad-spectrum insecticide use, which kills these beneficial insects too.
- **Chemical control:** use only when infestation is severe, following label rates; consider that repeated insecticide use can reduce natural predator populations and worsen aphid problems over time.

*Sources: ICRISAT/IITA groundnut production guides; A'Brook (1964) on aphid vectors and rosette disease.*
